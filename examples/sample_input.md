# The Case for Urban Vertical Farms

Cities import most of their fresh produce from hundreds of miles away, adding cost,
spoilage, and carbon emissions. Vertical farming grows crops in stacked indoor layers
inside the city itself.

## Why it matters
- Urban populations are growing; arable land near cities is shrinking.
- Long supply chains waste up to 30% of produce before it reaches shelves.
- Climate volatility makes open-field yields less predictable.

## How vertical farms work
Vertical farms use hydroponics or aeroponics under LED lighting, with tightly
controlled temperature, humidity, and nutrients. A single building can produce the
yield of many acres of farmland, using a fraction of the water.

## Benefits
- Year-round harvests independent of weather.
- 90%+ less water than conventional agriculture.
- Produce harvested the same day it is sold — fresher and less waste.
- Jobs and food security inside the community.

## Challenges
- High upfront capital and energy costs.
- Limited crop variety (leafy greens and herbs dominate today).
- Requires reliable, ideally renewable, electricity.

## The road ahead
Falling LED and renewable-energy costs are improving the economics every year.
Pilot projects in Singapore, Tokyo, and Newark show a credible path to scale.
The future of fresh food may be grown up, not out.
